<p>Table of Contents</p><h1><a id="Xee63f7d8ce839cb70e263cbb56e06b08d0f4d7a"></a>VACS Week 6 — Grand Rounds Capstone: Developer Guide</h1><p><strong>How to use this document.</strong> Give this whole document to the developer before they build the Week 6 feature. It is written in simple English for a developer whose first language is not English and who does not know clinical pharmacy. It explains what Week 6 is, what the student does, what the four content files are, how grading works, the data you must build, and the problems found in the uploaded files. Read <strong>Section 11 (Problems found in the uploaded files)</strong> early — some files are mislabeled and one file is missing.</p><h2><a id="plain-language-summary-one-paragraph"></a>1. Plain-language summary (one paragraph)</h2><p>Weeks 1 to 5 of VACS train the student by letting them <strong>talk to simulated patients</strong> and write short clinical notes. <strong>Week 6 is different.</strong> It is the final test (“capstone”). The student does <strong>not</strong> see a patient. Instead, the student writes one <strong>long written report</strong> about a medicine, called a <strong>“Grand Rounds presentation.”</strong> The app then <strong>grades that report</strong> against a fixed answer key (a “rubric”), gives <strong>written feedback</strong>, and asks a few <strong>follow-up questions</strong>. So Week 6 is a <em>document-submission-and-grading</em> feature, not a patient-interview feature.</p><p><a id="plain-language-summary-one-paragraph"></a></p><h2><a id="X4980874473afc889079b6040fcf9d082b6da19f"></a>2. Small glossary (only what the developer needs)</h2><p><strong>Clinical / education words</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Grand Rounds</strong> — a formal teaching talk that a pharmacist or doctor gives to other clinicians. Here the student writes it as a document instead of giving a live talk.</li><li><strong>Manuscript</strong> — the long written document the student submits. Think “report” or “article”.</li><li><strong>New Drug Review / Drug Monograph</strong> — a complete written summary of one medicine: what the disease is, how the drug works, the trial evidence, safety, dosing, and how to use it.</li><li><strong>MASH / MASLD</strong> — the liver disease in the Week 6 example topic. (You do <strong>not</strong> need to understand it. It is just the subject of the report.)</li><li><strong>Resmetirom (Rezdiffra)</strong> — the medicine in the Week 6 example topic.</li><li><strong>Clinical trial</strong> — a research study testing a medicine. The example uses three required studies: <strong>MAESTRO-NASH</strong>, <strong>MAESTRO-NAFLD-1</strong>, and an <strong>AASLD guidance</strong> document.</li><li><strong>Guideline</strong> — official rules from an expert group on how to use a medicine.</li><li><strong>Rubric</strong> — the grading sheet. It lists every concept the report should contain, each with a point value. The points add up to <strong>100</strong>.</li><li><strong>AMA citation format</strong> — a specific way to write references (numbered superscripts).</li><li><strong>Preceptor</strong> — the senior pharmacist who teaches and grades. Here, an <strong>AI Preceptor</strong> (an AI model) plays this role.</li></ul><p><strong>Technical words</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Topic</strong> — one assignment subject (for example, “Resmetirom in MASH”). Each topic has its own four content files (Section 5).</li><li><strong>Concept-based grading</strong> — grading by <strong>meaning</strong>, not by exact words. The grader must give credit when the student expresses the right idea, even with different wording. (This is the opposite of the Week 1–5 keyword grader; see Section 6.)</li><li><strong>LLM / AI model</strong> — the large language model used to read the manuscript and grade it by concept.</li></ul><p><a id="X4980874473afc889079b6040fcf9d082b6da19f"></a></p><h2><a id="X92a7c047a5e205094f79cf7b86523e6fe9a1dfd"></a>3. What Week 6 is, and how it differs from Weeks 1–5</h2><table><thead><tr><th></th><th><p>Weeks 1–5</p></th><th><p>Week 6 (Capstone)</p></th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><p>What the student does</p></td><td><p>Interviews a simulated patient, writes a short SOAP note</p></td><td><p>Writes one long drug-review manuscript</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>Main skill tested</p></td><td><p>Gathering and documenting patient information</p></td><td><p>Reading research and reasoning about evidence</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>Input to grading</p></td><td><p>The student’s short note sections</p></td><td><p>The student’s full uploaded .docx manuscript</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>Grading method</p></td><td><p><strong>Keyword matching</strong> (does the text contain certain words/values)</p></td><td><p><strong>Concept mastery</strong> (does the text express the right ideas, in any wording)</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>Grader</p></td><td><p>A simple text matcher in the app</p></td><td><p>An <strong>AI Preceptor</strong> (an AI model acting as a senior clinical pharmacist)</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>Output</p></td><td><p>A score and missing-element list</p></td><td><p>A score, <strong>written feedback in four parts</strong>, and <strong>follow-up questions</strong></p></td></tr></tbody></table><p><strong>Key idea:</strong> Week 6 asks the student to <em>evaluate evidence and defend recommendations</em>, which is a higher skill than recalling a guideline. So the grader must judge <strong>reasoning</strong>, not words.</p><p><a id="X92a7c047a5e205094f79cf7b86523e6fe9a1dfd"></a></p><h2><a id="the-students-experience-four-components"></a>4. The student’s experience — four components</h2><p>Week 6 has four steps, in order.</p><ol><li><strong>Written manuscript (the student submits this).</strong> The student uploads one Microsoft Word file (.docx) — a complete drug-review report. There is <strong>no page limit</strong>; it should be deep enough for a 45–60 minute presentation.</li><li><strong>AI clinical evaluation.</strong> After submission, the <strong>AI Preceptor</strong> reads the manuscript and scores it against the topic’s <strong>rubric</strong> (the 100-point answer key). It compares the student’s ideas to the <strong>model answer</strong>, judging <strong>concepts</strong>, not exact words.</li><li><strong>Feedback generation.</strong> The AI writes <strong>individual written feedback</strong> in <strong>four parts</strong> (see Section 7).</li><li><strong>Clinical discussion questions.</strong> The AI then asks about <strong>ten minutes</strong> of follow-up questions, like the question-and-answer part after a real Grand Rounds talk. The student must <strong>defend</strong> their recommendations and <strong>apply</strong> the evidence to new situations.</li></ol><p><a id="the-students-experience-four-components"></a></p><h2><a id="X96f4c5ee79161f71393f5b4f4265a518f69c98e"></a>5. One topic = four content files (the instructional assets)</h2><p>Every Week 6 <strong>topic</strong> must come with <strong>four</strong> files. The student is <strong>assigned</strong> a topic by the administrator (see Section 9); the student does <strong>not</strong> choose it.</p><table><thead><tr><th><p>Asset</p></th><th><p>Who sees it</p></th><th><p>What it is</p></th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><p><strong>Student Assignment Guide</strong></p></td><td><p>Student</p></td><td><p>The instructions: objectives, formatting, required articles, required sections, how to submit. This is the student’s handout.</p></td></tr><tr><td><p><strong>Model Answer</strong></p></td><td><p><strong>Instructor / AI only — never the student</strong></p></td><td><p>A complete, ideal version of the manuscript. It is the AI’s “source of truth” for grading.</p></td></tr><tr><td><p><strong>Granular Rubric (Answer Key)</strong></p></td><td><p>Instructor / AI only</p></td><td><p>The 100-point grading sheet (Section 6).</p></td></tr><tr><td><p><strong>Post-Presentation Discussion Guide</strong></p></td><td><p>Instructor / AI only</p></td><td><p>A bank of follow-up questions with model answers (Section 8).</p></td></tr></tbody></table><h3>The example topic provided: “Resmetirom (Rezdiffra) in MASH”</h3><ul><li><strong>Student Assignment Guide</strong> content includes:<ul><li><strong>Deliverable:</strong> one .docx manuscript, uploaded to the VACS site by end of Week 6.</li><li><strong>Formatting:</strong> Arial 11-point, single-spaced, 1-inch margins, <strong>AMA</strong> numbered citations, a references page at the end, no page limit, tables/figures allowed.</li><li><strong>Audience:</strong> a pharmacy department Grand Rounds (students, pharmacists, faculty).</li><li><strong>Three required articles</strong> that must be discussed: <strong>MAESTRO-NASH</strong> (efficacy trial), <strong>MAESTRO-NAFLD-1</strong> (safety trial), and the <strong>AASLD Practice Guidance</strong> (how to use the drug). Extra sources are allowed but not required.</li><li><strong>Seven required manuscript sections:</strong> (1) Disease State Background, (2) Pharmacology,<ol><li>Pivotal Efficacy Trial, (4) Safety Trial, (5) Clinical Implementation,</li><li>Place in Therapy and Formulary Recommendation, (7) Patient Counseling and Ambulatory Care Pharmacy Application.</li></ol></li></ul></li><li><strong>Model Answer</strong> content: a full reference manuscript (about 6,600 words) marked <strong>“FOR AI PRECEPTOR CALIBRATION ONLY — NOT FOR STUDENT DISTRIBUTION.”</strong> Never show it to students.</li></ul><p><a id="X96f4c5ee79161f71393f5b4f4265a518f69c98e"></a></p><h2><a id="the-grading-model-the-100-point-rubric"></a>6. The grading model (the 100-point rubric)</h2><p>The rubric is the <strong>answer key</strong>. It has the <strong>same shape</strong> as the Week 1–5 rubrics (sections → subsections → discrete weighted inputs), but it is <strong>bigger</strong> and is graded <strong>by concept</strong>.</p><p><strong>Structure of the example rubric — 8 sections, 100 points total:</strong></p><table><thead><tr><th><p>Section</p></th><th><p>Title</p></th><th><p>Points</p></th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td><p>1</p></td><td><p>Disease State Background</p></td><td><p>10</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>2</p></td><td><p>Pharmacology</p></td><td><p>10</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>3</p></td><td><p>Pivotal Efficacy Trial (MAESTRO-NASH)</p></td><td><p>25</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>4</p></td><td><p>Safety Trial (MAESTRO-NAFLD-1)</p></td><td><p>15</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>5</p></td><td><p>Clinical Implementation (AASLD Guidance)</p></td><td><p>15</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>6</p></td><td><p>Place in Therapy and Formulary Recommendation</p></td><td><p>10</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>7</p></td><td><p>Patient Counseling and Ambulatory Care Application</p></td><td><p>10</p></td></tr><tr><td><p>8</p></td><td><p>Presentation Quality (organization, formatting, citations)</p></td><td><p>5</p></td></tr><tr><td></td><td><p><strong>Total</strong></p></td><td><p><strong>100</strong></p></td></tr></tbody></table><ul><li>Each section is divided into <strong>subsections</strong> (for example 3A, 3B, 3C), and each subsection contains <strong>discrete inputs</strong> — one specific concept each.</li><li>The example rubric has <strong>280 discrete inputs</strong> in total.</li><li><strong>Each input</strong> has: an <strong>id</strong> (e.g., 3A.19), the <strong>concept text</strong>, a <strong>point value</strong>, and three written criteria — <strong>Full Credit</strong>, <strong>Half Credit</strong>, <strong>Zero Credit</strong> — that describe what earns each level. This supports <strong>partial credit</strong>.</li><li>The model answer scores <strong>100 / 100</strong> against this rubric (it is the reference).</li></ul><p><strong>The grading rule that is different from Weeks 1–5 (very important):</strong></p><ul><li>Weeks 1–5 grade by <strong>keyword matching</strong> (the app checks if the note text contains certain words or numbers).</li><li>Week 6 must grade by <strong>concept mastery</strong>. The <strong>AI Preceptor</strong> reads the manuscript and decides, <strong>for each rubric input</strong>, whether the student expressed the concept well enough for Full, Half, or Zero credit — <strong>even if the wording is different</strong> from the model answer.</li><li>The AI must <strong>not</strong> reward extra length, and must <strong>accept equivalent clinical reasoning</strong>.</li><li>So the grading engine is an <strong>AI/LLM evaluation</strong>, not the simple text matcher used in Weeks 1–5.</li></ul><p><a id="the-grading-model-the-100-point-rubric"></a></p><h2><a id="the-feedback-model-four-parts"></a>7. The feedback model (four parts)</h2><p>After scoring, the AI writes <strong>narrative (written) feedback</strong>, like an experienced preceptor. It must always have <strong>these four parts</strong>:</p><ol><li><strong>What Was Done Well</strong> — accurate evidence interpretation, good organization, sound reasoning.</li><li><strong>Opportunities for Improvement</strong> — missing concepts, incomplete evidence evaluation, weak justification.</li><li><strong>Critical Omissions</strong> — high-impact errors that could affect patient care or the recommendation. (These are the most serious misses.)</li><li><strong>Clinical Pearls</strong> — extra teaching points that go beyond the assignment.</li></ol><p><a id="the-feedback-model-four-parts"></a></p><h2><a id="Xe537f9230f56d72cc382e399eba1320aaea6f32"></a>8. The discussion questions (the follow-up Q&amp;A)</h2><ul><li>After feedback, the AI runs a short <strong>question-and-answer</strong> session (about ten minutes).</li><li>The questions are <strong>higher-order</strong>: the student must <strong>defend</strong> recommendations, <strong>interpret study limitations</strong>, <strong>justify</strong> decisions, <strong>apply</strong> findings to new patients, and <strong>discuss controversial</strong> areas.</li><li>The AI should <strong>choose questions dynamically</strong> based on what the student wrote (for example, ask about a section the student handled weakly).</li><li>Each question in the bank should store: the <strong>question text</strong>, a <strong>model answer</strong>, the <strong>concepts it tests</strong>, and optionally a <strong>difficulty</strong>.</li></ul><p><a id="Xe537f9230f56d72cc382e399eba1320aaea6f32"></a></p><h2><a id="X3522721030660a4565c445c6d68bbd0251cddf3"></a>9. Topic library and administrator assignment</h2><ul><li>Topics live in an <strong>administrator-maintained library</strong>. New topics can come from past Grand Rounds, new clinical topics, new drug approvals, or guideline updates.</li><li>The <strong>course administrator (Lisa)</strong> assigns each student a topic. Students do <strong>not</strong> pick their own topic. This keeps grading consistent and avoids duplicates.</li><li>So the app needs an <strong>admin function</strong> to: add a topic (with its four files), and <strong>assign</strong> a topic to a student.</li></ul><p><a id="X3522721030660a4565c445c6d68bbd0251cddf3"></a></p><h2><a id="the-data-model-the-developer-must-build"></a>10.    requiredSections[ { number, title, expectations } ],<br />    submissionInstructions<br />  }<br /><br />  modelAnswer                 // INSTRUCTOR/AI ONLY — never sent to the student<br /><br />  rubric {                    // INSTRUCTOR/AI ONLY<br />    totalPoints = 100<br />    sections[ {<br />      id, title, points,<br />      subsections[ {<br />        id, title, points,<br /> criticalOmissions, clinicalPearls }<br />  },<br />  discussion[ { questionId, studentAnswer, aiFollowUp } ]<br />}</p><p><strong>How the AI evaluation step works (inputs and / zero) and points for <strong>each rubric input</strong>, the <strong>section and total scores</strong>, and the <strong>four-part feedback</strong>.</li><li>The AI acts as a <strong>residency-trained ambulatory care pharmacist</strong> and grades <strong>concepts</strong>, not words.</li></ul><p><a id="the-data-model-the-developer-must-build"></a></p><h2><a Rules and constraints</h2><ul><li><strong>Never show the student</strong> the model answer, the rubric criteria, or the discussion answer key.</li><li><strong>Grade by concept, not by keyword.</strong> Accept correct reasoning in different words. Do not give extra points for length.</li><li>The rubric <strong>must total exactly 100 points</strong>, and the model answer must score <strong>100/100</strong> against it (use this to test your grader).</li><li>The AI must behave like a <strong>senior clinical pharmacist</strong> and focus on clinical accuracy, critical thinking, evidence synthesis, guideline application, judgment, and professional communication.</li><li>Do <strong>not</strong> change the clinical content of the four files; load it faithfully.</li><li>This module is <strong>separate</strong> from the Week 1–5 patient-interview and SOAP-note features. Reuse the look and feel, but build new screens for submission, grading, feedback, and discussion.</li></ul>
